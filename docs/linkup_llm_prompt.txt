Link app description for LLMs

LinkUp is a gay dating/social app meant to feel immediately different from Grindr-style apps: free core experience, less paywall hostility, more real connection, and monetization through ancillary commerce, sponsorships, ads, events, and partnerships rather than restricting basic dating functionality.

Architecturally, LinkUp should be client-heavy and server-light, not purely serverless for ideology’s sake. Servers or home authorities still handle identity, permissions, profile visibility, blocks, reports, abuse controls, exact-location protection, capability issuance, paid feature state, durable reconciliation, and fallback. Clients should do as much as practical, especially local computation, caching, profile rendering, and authorized peer communication.

Location is the core primitive. LinkUp uses density-adaptive geocells: social proximity units, not fixed map tiles. A geocell should approximate the people who feel immediately nearby, like the first page of a Grindr-style grid. Dense areas get smaller geocells; rural areas get larger ones. Discovery expands from current geocell to adjacent geocells to neighborhood to city to metro.

Locality-centered design applies everywhere: discovery, rendezvous, caching, replicated storage, bandwidth sharing, abuse detection, routing hints, backup placement, and recovery. The storage rule is “global addressability, local availability.”

Data is split into public, private, and internal. Public means user-approved/shareable subject to audience rules, not globally visible. Private means user-owned data the app may use but should not expose to other users. Internal means operational/system state. Internal data splits into static reference data like geodata and dynamic operational state like rendezvous, logs, moderation queues, replication cursors, and reconciliation state.

For storage, LinkUp may use an IPFS-like content-addressed replicated layer, but locality-aware rather than global-uniform. Static internal data is ideal for signed manifests and content hashes. Dynamic data should use append-only logs, signed checkpoints, replicated backups, and authoritative reconciliation. Nodes may carry/cache/relay/restore data, but they do not define truth.

For transport, WebRTC/RTCDataChannel is the practical peer transport for authorized app data/media, with signaling over WebSocket/HTTPS/XMPP/Matrix as needed. TURN-only relay can be used for privacy-sensitive modes. WebRTC should not be used as unrestricted broadcast. Peer-floated state should use signed, scoped, expiring, audience-encrypted envelopes/capabilities and append-only signed events. Truth comes from signatures, capabilities, expiry, audience encryption, and reconciliation.
